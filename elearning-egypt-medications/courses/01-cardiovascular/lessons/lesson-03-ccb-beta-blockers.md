## 1. Calcium channel blockers — mechanism

### 1.1 Pharmacology

CCBs block L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle and myocardium:

```
Ca²⁺ influx blocked → Smooth muscle relaxation → Vasodilation → ↓ BP
Dihydropyridines (amlodipine, nifedipine): Vascular selective → peripheral vasodilation
Non-DHP (verapamil, diltiazem): Cardiac + vascular → ↓ HR, ↓ contractility + vasodilation
```

**Evidence:** ALLHAT trial — amlodipine equivalent to chlorthalidone for CV outcomes. ASCOT-BPLA — amlodipine + perindopril superior to atenolol + thiazide for stroke prevention.

## 2. Beta-blockers — mechanism

### 2.1 Pharmacology

```
β1 blockade → ↓ HR, ↓ contractility, ↓ renin release → ↓ BP
β1 selective (bisoprolol, metoprolol): Cardioselective — less bronchospasm
Non-selective (propranolol): Blocks β1 + β2 — avoid in asthma
```

**Evidence:** COMET trial — carvedilol superior to metoprolol tartrate in HF mortality. Post-MI beta-blockers reduce mortality (NNT ~42 over 2 years).

### 2.3 Beta-blocker — contraindications & interactions

| Contraindication | Interaction |
|---|---|
| Severe bradycardia, 2°/3° AV block | Verapamil/diltiazem + BB → severe bradycardia |
| Decompensated HF | BB + insulin → masked hypoglycemia |
| Severe asthma (non-selective BB) | Abrupt BB withdrawal → rebound angina/tachycardia |

## 3. Clinical selection guide

| Situation | Preferred class |
|---|---|
| Uncomplicated HTN | CCB (amlodipine) — equal first line |
| HTN + angina | Beta-blocker if high HR; CCB if vasospastic |
| HFrEF | Beta-blocker (bisoprolol, carvedilol) — evidence-based |
| HTN + asthma | CCB preferred; cardioselective BB if needed |
| Post-MI | Beta-blocker mandatory unless contraindicated |
| Peripheral edema on CCB | Add ACE-I or switch to beta-blocker |

## 4. Pharmacy practice scenario

### Scenario 1.3 — Ankle swelling on amlodipine
> *Patient 58 years on amlodipine 10mg (Norvasc) for 3 months. BP 130/80 but bilateral ankle edema. No dyspnea.*

**Response:**
1. Confirm pitting edema — typical amlodipine effect (dose-dependent)
2. Screen for HF (weight, JVP, dyspnea) — refer if positive
3. Counsel leg elevation, salt reduction, compression stockings
4. Suggest physician review — ACE-I add-on often reduces CCB edema
5. Do NOT dispense furosemide OTC without prescription

---

## Key takeaways

> 📌 **Key Takeaways — Lesson 1.3**
> - Norvasc/Amlor (amlodipine) — most prescribed CCB in Egypt; generics 30–80 EGP/month
> - CCB edema is common; ACE-I co-prescription often helps
> - Concor (bisoprolol) and Lopressor (metoprolol) — key Egyptian beta-blocker brands
> - Never combine verapamil + beta-blocker without specialist oversight
> - Beta-blockers NOT first-line for uncomplicated HTN unless specific indication
> - Never stop beta-blockers abruptly — taper over 1–2 weeks
