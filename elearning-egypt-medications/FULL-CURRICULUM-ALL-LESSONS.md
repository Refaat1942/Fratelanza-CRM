# Lesson 1.1: Hypertension — Science, Guidelines & Egyptian Practice

## Learning objectives

After completing this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Define hypertension using **ESC/ESH 2023** and Egyptian guidelines
2. State **cardiovascular risk factors** prevalent in Egyptian population
3. Describe the **stepwise treatment algorithm** for uncomplicated HTN
4. List **first-line drug classes** with Egyptian brand examples
5. Counsel a patient on **lifestyle modification** in Arabic

---

## 1. Definition and epidemiology

### 1.1 Blood pressure classification (ESC 2023)

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|---|---|---|
| Optimal | <120 | <80 |
| Normal | 120–129 | 80–84 |
| High normal | 130–139 | 85–89 |
| **Grade 1 HTN** | 140–159 | 90–99 |
| **Grade 2 HTN** | 160–179 | 100–109 |
| **Grade 3 HTN** | ≥180 | ≥110 |
| Isolated systolic HTN | ≥140 | <90 |

**Egyptian prevalence data:**
- Adult hypertension: **26.3%** (WHO STEPS Egypt, various surveys 2017–2022)
- Awareness: ~50% know they have HTN
- Treatment: ~40% on medication
- Control: only **~20%** achieve target BP — **major pharmacy intervention opportunity**

### 1.2 Cardiovascular risk in Egypt

| Risk factor | Prevalence/significance |
|---|---|
| Obesity | Rising — 35%+ overweight/obese adults |
| Diabetes | 15.6% (IDF Diabetes Atlas Egypt) — doubles CV risk |
| Smoking | ~20% adult males |
| Salt intake | High — Egyptian diet (pickled foods, processed) |
| Physical inactivity | Urban lifestyle |
| Family history | Common presentation in pharmacy |

**Risk calculation:** Use SCORE2 or Egyptian adaptation for 10-year CV mortality risk.

---

## 2. Treatment targets

### 2.1 BP targets (ESC 2023 / Egyptian practice)

| Patient group | Target BP (mmHg) |
|---|---|
| General population <65 years | Systolic 120–129 (if tolerated) |
| General — practical Egypt target | **<140/90** |
| Age ≥65 years | Systolic 130–139 |
| Diabetes | **<130/80** (if tolerated) |
| CKD | <140/90 |
| Post-MI / heart failure | <130/80 |

### 2.2 Non-pharmacological management (FIRST LINE)

**Counseling script (Arabic):**

> "ضغطك محتاج علاج بنظام حياة قبل أو مع الدواء:
> 1. قلل الملح — ماتزودش الملح على الأكل، قلل المخللات والسلطة المخللة
> 2. امشي 30 دقيقة يومياً
> 3. قلل الوزن — كل 1 كجم بنقص الضغط حوالي 1 ملم زئبق
> 4. بطلي سجاير لو بتدخني
> 5. قلل التوتر والقلق
> التغييرات دي ممكن تنقص الضغط 5–10 وحدات لوحدها."

**Evidence:** DASH diet reduces SBP by **8–14 mmHg**; weight loss 5kg reduces SBP by **4–5 mmHg** (JNC 8 meta-analysis).

## 3. Pharmacological algorithm — Egyptian practice

### 3.1 First-line agents (all 4 classes equal per NICE/ESC)

```
Step 1: Choose ONE of:
├── ACE inhibitor (e.g., ramipril) OR ARB (e.g., losartan)
├── Calcium channel blocker (e.g., amlodipine)
├── Thiazide diuretic (e.g., HCTZ 12.5–25mg)
└── (Beta-blocker — NOT first line unless specific indication)

Step 2 (if not at target): Combine TWO from different classes
Common Egyptian FDC products at Step 2

Step 3: Triple therapy — ACE/ARB + CCB + diuretic
```

## 4. Pharmacy practice scenarios

### Scenario 1.1 — New diagnosis
> *Patient 52 years, BP 155/95 measured at pharmacy. No medication. Asks what to do.*

**Response:**
1. Confirm measurement (seated 5 min, repeat ×3)
2. Recommend physician visit for diagnosis and workup (ECG, labs: creatinine, K+, lipids, glucose)
3. Counsel lifestyle modifications (script above)
4. Do NOT start antihypertensive without prescription
5. Offer BP monitoring log card

### Scenario 1.2 — Resistant hypertension
> *Patient on amlodipine 10mg + ramipril 10mg + HCTZ 25mg. BP still 150/90.*

**Response:**
1. Assess adherence (ask open questions: "كام مرة فاتتك الجرعة الأسبوع ده؟")
2. Check for NSAID use (ibuprofen very common in Egypt — raises BP)
3. Check for licorice, oral contraceptives, decongestants
4. Refer to physician for resistant HTN workup (secondary causes: renal artery stenosis, primary aldosteronism, OSA)

---

## 5. Key takeaways

> 📌 **Key Takeaways — Lesson 1.1**
> - Egypt HTN prevalence ~26%; only ~20% controlled
> - Target <140/90 general; <130/80 diabetes/high-risk
> - 4 equal first-line classes: ACE-I/ARB, CCB, thiazide, (beta-blocker if indicated)
> - Lifestyle counseling is FIRST — DASH diet, salt reduction, exercise
> - Egyptian market has extensive generic options at lower cost
> - NSAID use is a common hidden cause of uncontrolled HTN in Egypt

## 1. Mechanism of action

### 1.1 RAAS pathway

```
Renin (kidney) → Angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I
    → ACE (lung/vessels) → Angiotensin II
    → Vasoconstriction + Aldosterone release
    → Na+/water retention + BP elevation + cardiac remodeling
```

**ACE inhibitors** block angiotensin-converting enzyme → ↓ Angiotensin II → vasodilation, ↓ aldosterone.

**ARBs** block AT1 receptor → same downstream effect without affecting bradykinin (less cough).

### 1.2 Clinical effects (evidence)

| Outcome | Evidence | NNT (5 years) |
|---|---|---|
| BP reduction | ACE-I: SBP ↓ 8–10 mmHg | — |
| Heart failure mortality | SOLVD, CONSENSUS trials | 1 in 6 lives saved |
| Post-MI remodeling | SAVE, AIRE trials | Significant LV function preservation |
| Diabetic nephropathy | RENAAL (losartan) | Slows proteinuria progression |
| Stroke prevention | PROGRESS (perindopril) | 1 in 28 for recurrent stroke |

### 2.2 ACE inhibitor side effects

| Effect | Frequency | Management |
|---|---|---|
| Dry cough | 5–20% | Switch to ARB |
| Hyperkalemia | 3–5% | Monitor K+; avoid K+ supplements, K-sparing diuretics |
| Angioedema | 0.1–0.7% | **STOP immediately** — medical emergency |
| First-dose hypotension | Captopril > others | Take first dose at bedtime; start low dose |
| Renal function change | Expected initially | Monitor creatinine — acceptable rise <30% |
| Teratogenic (pregnancy) | Category D | **Contraindicated** — counsel women of childbearing age |

### 3.2 ACE-I vs ARB — when to choose

| Situation | Choice |
|---|---|
| New HTN, no contraindication | ACE-I (cheaper generics in Egypt) |
| ACE cough develops | Switch to ARB (same class benefit) |
| Post-MI with LV dysfunction | ACE-I preferred (more evidence) |
| Diabetic nephropathy | Either — losartan/irbesartan strong evidence |
| Pregnancy | **Neither** — contraindicated |
| Hyperkalemia risk | Use cautiously; monitor K+ both classes |
| Cannot tolerate either | CCB or thiazide |

**NEVER combine ACE-I + ARB** — increased harm (hyperkalemia, renal failure) without benefit (ONTARGET trial).

## 4. Renal dosing

| eGFR (mL/min) | ACE-I/ARB action |
|---|---|
| >60 | Full dose |
| 30–60 | Start low; monitor creatinine in 1–2 weeks |
| <30 | Use cautiously; nephrology referral; some avoid |
| Dialysis | Losartan, telmisartan — some dialyzable |

**Rule:** Acceptable creatinine rise **<30%** from baseline after starting ACE-I/ARB. If >30%, investigate renal artery stenosis.

## 1. Calcium channel blockers — mechanism

### 1.1 Pharmacology

CCBs block L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle and myocardium:

```
Ca²⁺ influx blocked → Smooth muscle relaxation → Vasodilation → ↓ BP
Dihydropyridines (amlodipine, nifedipine): Vascular selective → peripheral vasodilation
Non-DHP (verapamil, diltiazem): Cardiac + vascular → ↓ HR, ↓ contractility + vasodilation
```

**Evidence:** ALLHAT trial — amlodipine equivalent to chlorthalidone for CV outcomes. ASCOT-BPLA — amlodipine + perindopril superior to atenolol + thiazide for stroke prevention.

## 2. Beta-blockers — mechanism

**Evidence:** COMET trial — carvedilol superior to metoprolol tartrate in HF mortality. Post-MI beta-blockers reduce mortality (NNT ~42 over 2 years).

## 3. Comprehensive case scenarios

### Case 1 — Diabetic hypertensive (common Egyptian presentation)
> *58-year-old male. BP 158/92. HbA1c 8.2%. LDL 145 mg/dL. No prior CV events. Currently on metformin only.*

**Pharmacy assessment:**
- Very high CV risk (diabetes + HTN + dyslipidemia)
- Recommend physician visit for: ACE-I/ARB + statin + possibly SGLT2i
- Suggest targets: BP <130/80, LDL <55–70 mg/dL
- Counsel lifestyle: salt, exercise, smoking cessation
- Cost-conscious option: Tareg generic + Ator (Amoun) + amlodipine generic

### Case 2 — Post-MI polypharmacy
> *62-year-old female. Post-MI 3 months. Medications: Aspocid, Plavix, Liptior 40mg, Concor 5mg, Ramipril 5mg, Lasix 20mg PRN.*

**Key checks:**
- DAPT duration — confirm with cardiologist before stopping Plavix
- Beta-blocker and ACE-I — lifelong unless contraindicated
- Statin high-intensity — LDL target <55 mg/dL
- Warn: ibuprofen (Brufen) contraindicated — GI bleed + cardioprotection loss
- Arabic counseling on adherence to ALL drugs — stopping Concor abruptly is dangerous

### Case 3 — AF on warfarin with new prescription
> *70-year-old male. Marevan 5mg, INR 2.3. New prescription: Ciprobay (ciprofloxacin) for UTI.*

**Action:**
- Fluoroquinolones may ↑ or ↓ INR unpredictably
- Physician notification for closer INR monitoring
- Counsel bleeding and clot signs
- No OTC NSAIDs

### Case 4 — Resistant HTN hidden causes
> *45-year-old. Amlodipine 10mg + losartan 100mg + HCTZ 25mg. BP 162/98. Takes Brufen daily, licorice tea, decongestant for "cold."*

**Pharmacy intervention:**
1. Identify NSAID + decongestant (pseudoephedrine) — both raise BP
2. Licorice → pseudoaldosteronism
3. Counsel stopping OTC offenders
4. Refer for secondary HTN workup if still uncontrolled

### Case 5 — Heart failure decompensation
> *68-year-old. Known HFrEF. Weight up 4kg in 5 days, ankle swelling, shortness of breath. On Tritace, Concor, Aldactone, Lasix 40mg OD.*

**Urgent action:**
1. Recognize decompensation — refer to physician/hospital SAME DAY
2. Do NOT advise stopping any GDMT drug
3. May suggest physician contact for Lasix dose increase
4. Daily weight counseling for future

## Learning objectives

5. Counsel patients on **appropriate antibiotic use** and completion of course in Arabic

---

## 1. Antimicrobial stewardship framework

### 1.1 Core principles

```
Stewardship goals:
├── Improve patient outcomes
├── Reduce antimicrobial resistance (AMR)
├── Minimize adverse effects (C. difficile, nephrotoxicity)
└── Control healthcare costs
```

**WHO AWaRe classification:**

| Category | Meaning | Egyptian examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Access** | First/second choice — widely available | Amoxicillin, amoxicillin-clavulanate, cephalexin, trimethoprim |
| **Watch** | Higher resistance risk — restrict use | Ciprofloxacin, azithromycin, ceftriaxone, clindamycin |
| **Reserve** | Last-line — specialist use | Meropenem, colistin, linezolid |

**Evidence:** WHO Global Action Plan on AMR (2015); Egyptian National AMR Action Plan aligned with WHO.

### 1.2 Egyptian resistance data (summary)

| Pathogen | Resistance concern (Egypt surveillance) |
|---|---|
| E. coli (UTI) | ESBL production 30–50% in hospital isolates; high fluoroquinolone resistance |
| Klebsiella | Carbapenem resistance emerging in ICU settings |
| S. aureus | MRSA 20–40% in some hospital surveys |
| S. pneumoniae | Penicillin/macrolide resistance variable |
| M. tuberculosis | MDR-TB ~3% new cases (WHO Egypt estimate) |

### 1.3 EDA regulatory framework

| Regulation | Pharmacy implication |
|---|---|
| Prescription-only antimicrobials | Must not dispense without valid Rx |
| Retention of Rx copies | 2 years minimum per Egyptian pharmacy law |
| Controlled antimicrobials (TB, some IV) | Hospital/specialist channels |
| OTC misuse concern | Nifuroxazide, some antidiarrheals sold OTC — stewardship issue |

**Counseling script (Arabic):**

> "المضاد الحيوي بيعالج البكتيريا بس، مش الفيروسات زي البرد والإنفلونزا. لازم تكمل الكورس كله حتى لو حسيت بتحسن. متشاركش المضاد مع حد تاني ومتستخدمش مضاد قديم من عندك. ده بيخلي البكتيريا تقاوم الدوا."

---

## 2. Pharmacist stewardship interventions

| Intervention | Example |
|---|---|
| Validate indication | Question fluoroquinolone for uncomplicated UTI when nitrofurantoin/cephalexin appropriate |
| Duration check | Amoxicillin 5–7 days for otitis; not 14 days routinely |
| DDI screening | Macrolide + statin → myopathy; metronidazole + warfarin |
| Patient education | Arabic counseling on adherence and resistance |
| Referral | Persistent fever >48h on antibiotics → physician |

---

## 3. Pharmacy practice scenario

### Scenario 2.1 — Viral URI antibiotic request
> *Mother requests amoxicillin for 4-year-old with runny nose and cough 2 days, no fever. Says neighbor's child got same antibiotic.*

**Response:**
1. Explain viral etiology — antibiotics not indicated
2. Recommend supportive care: saline nasal drops, paracetamol if fever, fluids
3. Red flags for referral: fever >39°C, difficulty breathing, >10 days symptoms
4. Do NOT dispense antibiotic without prescription regardless
5. Document counseling provided

---

## Key takeaways

> 📌 **Key Takeaways — EG-MED-02-L01**
> - AWaRe: Access antibiotics first line; Watch/Reserve need justification
> - Egyptian E. coli UTI: high FQ resistance — avoid empiric ciprofloxacin when possible
> - Pharmacists must enforce prescription-only rules — no antibiotic OTC dispensing
> - Nifuroxazide OTC misuse contributes to resistance concerns in Egypt
> - Complete course counseling prevents partial treatment resistance
> - Egyptian National AMR Plan — pharmacy is frontline stewardship

## 1. Mechanism & resistance

```
Beta-lactam ring → binds PBPs → inhibits cell wall synthesis → bactericidal
Beta-lactamase enzymes → hydrolyze ring → resistance
Clavulanate/sulbactam → beta-lactamase inhibitors → restore activity
```

**Evidence:** Amoxicillin-clavulanate remains first-line for many community infections per Egyptian MOH essential medicines list.

## 1. Macrolides

### 1.1 Mechanism & evidence

| Generic | Brand (Egypt) | Manufacturer | Dosing | Approx. EGP |

## 1. Mechanism

Sulfonylureas close K-ATP channels → insulin secretion independent of glucose → hypoglycemia risk.

| Drug | Brand (Egypt) | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gliclazide MR | Diamicron MR | 30–120mg OD | Lower hypoglycemia vs glibenclamide |
| Glimepiride | Amaryl | 1–4mg OD | Once daily |
| Glibenclamide | Glibenclamide | 2.5–5mg BD | **High hypoglycemia** — avoid elderly |
| Glipizide | Minidiab | 5–10mg | Short acting |

## 2. Hypoglycemia management — Rule of 15

```
BG <70 mg/dL → 15g fast carb (juice, honey, 3 glucose tabs)
→ Wait 15 min → Recheck → Repeat if still low
→ Eat meal once stable
Severe: glucagon IM or emergency services
```

**Counseling script (Arabic):**
> "السلفونيل يوريا بينزل السكر بقوة — لازم تاكل بانتظام وماتفوتش وجبة. لو حسيت عرق بارد، رعشة، جوع شديد — قيس السكر فوراً وكل عسل أو عصير. الكحول خطر مع الدوا ده."

## 3. Scenario
> *Elderly patient on glibenclamide 5mg BD found confused at pharmacy. Pale, sweating.*

**Response:** Treat as hypoglycemia — give 15g glucose immediately, call emergency if not improving. Counsel physician to switch from glibenclamide to gliclazide MR.
