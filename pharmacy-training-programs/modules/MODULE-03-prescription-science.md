## Section 3.1 — Prescription as a legal and clinical document

### 3.1.1 Legal requirements (Egypt)

A valid prescription in Egypt must contain:

| Element | Arabic | Legal basis |
|---|---|---|
| Patient full name | اسم المريض | Pharmacy practice regulations |
| Patient age (especially pediatrics) | العمر | Dose calculation requirement |
| Prescriber name and signature | اسم الطبيب وتوقيعه | Accountability |
| Prescriber license/registration number | رقم القيد | EDA prescriber registry |
| Date of prescription | التاريخ | Validity period |
| Drug name (generic or brand) | اسم الدواء | Identification |
| Dose, frequency, route, duration | الجرعة والتكرار والمدة | Safe use |
| Prescriber stamp (clinic/hospital) | ختم العيادة | Authentication |

**Prescription validity period:** Generally **7–14 days** from date of issue for acute medications; chronic medications may have longer validity with repeat authorization. Controlled substances: stricter rules, no refills without new prescription.

### 3.1.2 Types of prescriptions

| Type | Characteristics | Pharmacy action |
|---|---|---|
| Private outpatient | Doctor's clinic stamp | Standard verification |
| Hospital discharge | Hospital letterhead | Verify discharge plan, interactions with home meds |
| Electronic (e-Rx) | Digital signature, QR code | Verify authenticity, emerging in Egypt |
| Emergency | Verbal order followed by written within 24h | Document verbal order details, confirm in writing |
| Controlled (Schedule) | Special form, quantity in words + figures | Register in controlled drug book |

## Section 3.1.3 — Latin abbreviations — complete reference

**High-error abbreviations (ISMP "Do Not Use" list):**

| ❌ Do NOT use | ✅ Use instead | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| U | unit | Units (insulin) — confused with "0" |
| IU | international units | Spell out |
| QD, QOD | daily, every other day | Confused with each other |
| Trailing zero (1.0 mg) | 1 mg | Decimal error |

## Section 3.2 — Systematic clinical checking

### 3.2.1 The 7-step prescription review (evidence-based protocol)

Adapted from **NICE NG5 (Medicines Optimisation)** and **ASHP guidelines:**

| Step | Check | Question to ask |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Indication** | Is there a drug for this condition? Is the drug appropriate for the indication? |
| 2 | **Identity** | Correct patient? Age/weight appropriate? |
| 3 | **Drug selection** | Right drug class? Contraindications? Allergies? |
| 4 | **Dose** | Correct dose for age, weight, renal/hepatic function? |
| 5 | **Frequency & route** | Appropriate schedule? Correct route? |
| 6 | **Duration** | Appropriate length? Antibiotic course complete? |
| 7 | **Interactions & duplicates** | Drug-drug, drug-disease? Duplicate therapy? |

### 3.2.2 Dose rationality — worked examples

**Example 1 — Pediatric amoxicillin:**

> Rx: Amoxicillin suspension 250mg/5mL, 5mL TDS × 7 days. Patient: 3-year-old, 15 kg. Indication: otitis media.

**Check:**
- Recommended dose for otitis media: **40–45 mg/kg/day** (AAP/IDSA guidelines)
- Prescribed dose: 250mg × 3 = 750mg/day ÷ 15kg = **50 mg/kg/day**
- Assessment: **Slightly above range but acceptable** for severe infection. Duration 7 days appropriate (5–10 days for otitis media).
- Quantity: 5mL TDS × 7 days = 105mL → dispense **1 bottle (100mL) + advise to complete course**

**Example 2 — Adult metformin in renal impairment:**

> Rx: Metformin 1000mg BD. Patient: 72-year-old female, eGFR 38 mL/min (CKD stage 3b).

**Check:**
- Metformin contraindicated if eGFR <30; **caution 30–45** (FDA 2016 update)
- At eGFR 38: Maximum recommended = **1000mg/day** (not 2000mg)
- Action: **Contact prescriber** — recommend metformin 500mg BD or 1000mg OD; risk of lactic acidosis

**Lactate risk data:** Metformin-associated lactic acidosis incidence ~3–10 per 100,000 patient-years in approved use; mortality ~50% when it occurs (Salpeter et al., Cochrane 2010).

## Section 3.3 — Quantity calculation

### 3.3.1 Formulas

**Tablets/capsules:**
> Total quantity = (dose per administration) × (frequency per day) × (duration in days)

**Liquids:**
> Total volume (mL) = (volume per dose in mL) × (frequency per day) × (duration in days)

**Example:**
> Ciprofloxacin 500mg BD × 10 days = 500 × 2 × 10 = **20 tablets**
> Paracetamol syrup 120mg/5mL, 5mL QID PRN × 5 days max = 5 × 4 × 5 = **100mL**

### 3.3.2 Insulin prescription interpretation

> Rx: Insulin glargine 100 units/mL, 20 units at bedtime SC

**Check:**
- Units ≠ mL: 20 units = 0.2 mL (critical — never confuse)
- Dispense: pens or vials with syringes
- Counsel: injection technique, hypoglycemia recognition, storage (opened: room temp 28 days; unopened: refrigerate)
- **Never use "U" abbreviation** — ISMP high-alert

## Section 3.4 — Prescription error epidemiology

### 3.4.1 Types and frequency

**Bates et al. (1995, JAMA)** — landmark study on medication errors:

| Error type | % of total (hospital) | Preventable by pharmacist |
|---|---|---|
| Prescribing errors | 39% | Yes — clinical checking |
| Administration errors | 38% | Partially — counseling |
| Dispensing errors | 12% | Yes — verification |
| Transcription errors | 11% | Yes — e-prescribing reduces |

**Community pharmacy dispensing error rate:** Estimated **0.01–5.0%** of items dispensed (James et al., 2009, *Research in Social and Administrative Pharmacy*), with **~33% potentially harmful**.

**Most common dispensing errors:**
1. Wrong drug (look-alike/sound-alike) — 30%
2. Wrong strength — 25%
3. Wrong quantity — 15%
4. Wrong label instructions — 20%
5. Omission — 10%

## Section 3.5 — Case studies

### CASE 3.1 — Multiple errors

> *Rx: Tab. Metformin 500mg TDS, Tab. Glibenclamide 5mg BD, Pt: Ahmed, 55y. No allergies noted.*

**Findings:**
1. Metformin 500mg TDS = 1500mg/day — acceptable
2. Glibenclamide 5mg BD in 55-year-old — **hypoglycemia risk** (prefer gliclazide in elderly)
3. Dual therapy without documented HbA1c — cannot verify indication from Rx alone
4. No duration specified — chronic therapy assumed
5. **Action:** Counsel on hypoglycemia signs; recommend glucose monitoring; suggest physician review for gliclazide switch

### CASE 3.2 — Pediatric overdose risk

> *Rx: Paracetamol 500mg tab, 2 tabs QID PRN, Age: 6 years, 20 kg*

**Findings:**
1. **WRONG formulation** — 500mg tablets inappropriate for 6-year-old
2. Dose if taken: 1000mg QID = 4000mg/day = **200 mg/kg/day** — **TOXIC** (max 60 mg/kg/day)
3. **Toxic dose threshold:** >150 mg/kg/day or >10g/day in adults
4. **Action: REFUSE TO DISPENSE.** Contact prescriber for paracetamol suspension 120mg/5mL, weight-based dosing (15 mg/kg/dose QID)

**Paracetamol toxicity science:** Hepatotoxic metabolite NAPQI exceeds glutathione conjugation capacity at toxic doses. N-acetylcysteine (NAC) antidote effective within 8 hours of ingestion (Rumack-Matthew nomogram).
