## Section 10.1 — Egyptian pharmacy legal framework

### 10.1.1 Key regulatory bodies

| Body | Arabic | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Egyptian Drug Authority (EDA) | هيئة الدواء المصرية | Drug registration, GMP inspection, pharmacovigilance |
| Ministry of Health and Population | وزارة الصحة والسكان | Health policy, pharmacy licensing |
| Egyptian Pharmacists Syndicate | نقابة الصيادلة المصرية | Professional registration, ethics, CPD |
| Central Administration of Pharmaceutical Affairs (CAPA) | الإدارة المركزية للشئون الصيدلية | Pharmacy licensing, inspection |

### 10.1.2 Pharmacy licensing requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| Licensed pharmacist on premises | Must be present during all operating hours |
| Pharmacy location license | Approved by health authority |
| EDA supplier license | Purchase only from authorized distributors |
| Controlled substance license | Additional permit for narcotic/psychotropic stocks |
| Record keeping | Purchase invoices, dispensing records, controlled drug register |

**Legal principle:** Dispensing prescription-only medication without valid prescription is a **criminal offense** under Egyptian pharmacy law.

## Section 10.2 — Controlled substances

### 10.2.1 Scheduling (international conventions + Egyptian law)

Egypt is signatory to:
- **Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs (1961)** — UN
- **Convention on Psychotropic Substances (1971)** — UN

| Schedule | Examples | Controls |
|---|---|---|
| Narcotic (Schedule I–II) | Morphine, fentanyl, oxycodone, codeine (high strength) | Strict register; prescriber license; quantity limits |
| Psychotropic (Schedule III–IV) | Benzodiazepines (diazepam, alprazolam), zolpidem, tramadol | Register; valid prescription; refill restrictions |
| Prescription-only (non-controlled) | Antibiotics, antihypertensives, antidepressants | Valid prescription required |

### 10.2.2 Controlled drug register requirements

Every entry must record:

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Date | Dispensing date |
| Patient name and address | Full identification |
| Prescriber name and license | Verification |
| Drug name, form, strength | Exact product |
| Quantity dispensed (words + figures) | Fraud prevention |
| Balance remaining in stock | Running total |
| Pharmacist signature | Accountability |
| Prescription reference number | Traceability |

**Daily stock check:** Physical count must match register balance. Discrepancies reported immediately.

### 10.2.3 Tramadol — Egypt-specific note

Tramadol has been subject to **enhanced controls** in Egypt due to abuse epidemic:
- Requires prescription
- Maximum quantity limits per prescription
- Registered in controlled substance monitoring
- Pharmacists must verify prescriber legitimacy

**Abuse data:** Tramadol misuse documented across Middle East and North Africa region; Egypt implemented stricter scheduling in response to public health crisis.

## Section 10.3 — Counterfeit drug detection

### 10.3.1 WHO counterfeit definition

> *"A product that is deliberately and fraudulently mislabeled with respect to identity and/or source."*

**Global scale:** WHO estimates **10% of medicines in low- and middle-income countries** are substandard or falsified.

### 10.3.2 Authentication checklist

| Check | Genuine | Suspect counterfeit |
|---|---|---|
| Packaging print quality | Sharp, consistent colors | Blurry, misaligned text |
| Hologram/security seal | Present, changes with angle | Missing or photocopied |
| Batch number + expiry | Clear, embossed or printed | Typo errors, missing |
| Price | Market-consistent | Suspiciously cheap (>30% below market) |
| Source | Authorized distributor invoice | No invoice, cash market purchase |
| Tablet appearance | Consistent color, shape, markings | Chipping, color variation, no markings |
| Dissolution | Normal disintegration | Does not dissolve, unusual taste |

**Action if suspected:** Quarantine product; report to EDA; do not dispense; document batch and supplier.

### 10.3.3 Mobile authentication (Ghana model / global trend)

Some countries implement **scratch-off SMS verification** codes on drug packages. Egypt EDA has explored serialization and track-and-trace systems — pharmacists should stay updated on EDA circulars.

## Section 10.4 — Professional liability

### 10.4.1 Negligence elements

| Element | Pharmacy example |
|---|---|
| **Duty of care** | Pharmacist owes standard of care to every patient |
| **Breach** | Dispensed wrong drug without verification |
| **Causation** | Patient harmed because of wrong drug |
| **Damages** | Injury, hospitalization, death |

**Standard of care:** What a **reasonably competent pharmacist** would do in the same situation (Bolam test).

### 10.4.2 Professional indemnity insurance

Recommended for all practicing pharmacists. Covers legal defense and compensation for claims arising from professional practice.

## Section 10.5 — Ethical scenarios (Egypt context)

### Scenario 10.1
*Doctor calls pharmacy asking what medications patient is taking. Doctor claims to be treating patient.*

**Action:** Verify doctor identity. Confirm patient has consented to information sharing. If emergency/life-threatening, disclosure may be justified under beneficence principle. Document the call.

### Scenario 10.2
*Relative asks for patient's medication list. Says they are the spouse.*

**Action:** Unless patient has given prior consent, **refuse**. Confidentiality applies to family members. Offer to contact patient directly for consent.

### Scenario 10.3
*Manager instructs staff to sell antibiotics without prescription during busy period.*

**Action:** Refuse. Document instruction. Report to Pharmacists Syndicate if persistent. This is illegal and contributes to AMR public health crisis.
