## Section 1.1 — History and evolution of pharmacy
## القسم 1.1 — تاريخ وتطور الصيدلة

### 1.1.1 From antiquity to modern practice

Pharmacy derives from the Greek *pharmakon* (φάρμακον), meaning both "drug" and "poison" — reflecting the dual nature of medicines as therapeutic agents with inherent risk.

**Historical milestones:**

| Era | Development | Scientific significance |
|---|---|---|
| Ancient Egypt (c. 1550 BCE) | Ebers Papyrus — 700+ drug formulas | First documented pharmaceutical formulary |
| Islamic Golden Age (9th–13th c.) | Al-Razi, Ibn Sina (Avicenna) — *Canon of Medicine* | Systematic pharmacology; distillation techniques |
| 19th century | Isolation of alkaloids (morphine 1804, quinine 1820) | Birth of pure chemical drugs |
| 20th century | Antibiotics (penicillin 1928), insulin (1921) | Transformation of mortality from infectious disease |
| 1990 | Hepler & Strand — "Pharmaceutical Care" concept | Shift from product-focused to patient-outcome-focused practice |
| 2017 | WHO Global Patient Safety Challenge: Medication Without Harm | International mandate for medication safety |

### 1.1.2 The pharmaceutical care model

**Hepler & Strand (1990)** defined pharmaceutical care as:

> *"The responsible provision of drug therapy for the purpose of achieving definite outcomes that improve a patient's quality of life."*

**Three core responsibilities:**

1. Identifying actual and potential drug-related problems (DRPs)
2. Resolving actual DRPs
3. Preventing potential DRPs

**Drug-Related Problem (DRP) categories (PCNE classification v9.0):**

| Code | Category | Example |
|---|---|---|
| P1 | Untreated indication | Hypertension diagnosed but no antihypertensive prescribed |
| P2 | Use without indication | Antibiotic for viral URI |
| P3 | Wrong drug | Beta-blocker in asthma patient |
| P4 | Dose too low | Metformin 250mg when 1000mg needed |
| P5 | Dose too high | Digoxin 0.25mg in elderly with renal impairment |
| P6 | Adverse drug reaction | ACE inhibitor causing persistent dry cough |
| P7 | Drug interaction | Warfarin + NSAID → bleeding |
| P8 | Wrong administration | Enteric-coated tablet crushed |
| P9 | Non-adherence | Patient stops statin after 2 weeks |

**Scientific evidence:** A meta-analysis by Chisholm-Burns et al. (2010, *Medical Care*) found pharmacist interventions reduced healthcare costs by **$12.15 per $1 invested** and improved medication adherence by **12.8%** across 298 studies.

## Section 1.2 — Pharmacy in the healthcare system
## القسم 1.2 — الصيدلية في المنظومة الصحية

### 1.2.1 WHO medication safety epidemiology

The **World Health Organization** launched the third Global Patient Safety Challenge in 2017: **Medication Without Harm**, with the goal of reducing severe, avoidable medication-related harm by **50%** within five years.

**Key global statistics (WHO, 2017):**

| Metric | Data |
|---|---|
| Annual cost of medication errors (global) | **US$ 42 billion** (nearly 1% of global health expenditure) |
| Medication errors — contributing factor in death | **1 in 131 outpatient deaths**, **1 in 854 inpatient deaths** |
| Hospital admissions due to medication harm | Estimated **4.3%** of total hospital admissions in some systems |
| Most error-prone stage | **Prescribing (39%)** and **administration (38%)** — dispensing accounts for ~12% |
| Patient non-adherence | **50%** of patients do not take medications as prescribed (WHO, 2003) |

**Implication for community pharmacy:** Even though dispensing errors represent ~12% of total medication errors, the community pharmacy is the **last line of defense** before the patient — making verification and counseling critical.

### 1.2.2 Pharmacy types and scope in Egypt

| Type | Primary function | Scientific focus |
|---|---|---|
| Community pharmacy | Dispensing, OTC counseling, chronic disease support | Patient-facing pharmacology, adherence science |
| Hospital pharmacy | Ward supply, IV preparation, clinical rounds | Sterile compounding, TPN, oncology protocols |
| Clinical pharmacy | Direct patient care on wards | Therapeutic drug monitoring, kinetics |
| Industrial pharmacy | Manufacturing, QC/QA | GMP, stability testing, formulation science |
| Regulatory pharmacy | EDA, drug registration | Pharmacovigilance, bioequivalence review |

**Egyptian context:** Egypt has approximately **70,000+ registered pharmacists** (Egyptian Pharmacists Syndicate estimates) serving a population of ~105 million — a ratio of approximately **1 pharmacist per 1,500 population**, compared to WHO recommended minimum of 1 per 2,000 for basic pharmacy services.

## Section 1.3 — Professional ethics
## القسم 1.3 — أخلاقيات المهنة

### 1.3.1 Beauchamp & Childress — Four principles

| Principle | Definition | Pharmacy application |
|---|---|---|
| **Autonomy** | Respect patient's right to self-determination | Provide information for informed consent; respect refusal |
| **Beneficence** | Act in patient's best interest | Recommend most effective, safest therapy |
| **Non-maleficence** | "First, do no harm" | Prevent dispensing errors, interactions, contraindicated drugs |
| **Justice** | Fair distribution of healthcare resources | Equitable service regardless of socioeconomic status |

### 1.3.2 Confidentiality — legal and ethical basis

**Hippocratic Oath principle:** *"What I see or hear in the lives of my patients... I will keep secret."*

**Practical rules:**
- Patient medication history is **protected health information**
- Discuss patient cases only with healthcare team members involved in care
- Never discuss patient purchases in public areas of pharmacy
- WhatsApp/phone counseling must verify patient identity before disclosing information

**Violation consequences in Egypt:** Professional disciplinary action by Pharmacists Syndicate; potential civil liability under Egyptian Civil Code for privacy breach.

### 1.3.3 Ethical dilemmas — case analysis framework

**CASE STUDY 1.1:**

*A customer asks you to sell amoxicillin without a prescription for their child's sore throat. They say they cannot afford a doctor visit.*

**Ethical analysis:**

| Principle | Analysis |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | Parent making decision for child — but lacks medical diagnosis |
| Beneficence | Antibiotic may help IF bacterial; harmful if viral (resistance, side effects) |
| Non-maleficence | Inappropriate antibiotic use drives **antimicrobial resistance (AMR)** — WHO lists AMR as top 10 global health threats |
| Justice | Denying access may seem unfair, but antibiotic misuse harms entire population |

**WHO AMR data:** Antimicrobial resistance could cause **10 million deaths annually by 2050** if unchecked (O'Neill Review on AMR, 2016). Inappropriate community antibiotic dispensing is a **major driver** of resistance in low- and middle-income countries.

**Recommended action:**
1. Explain that sore throat is viral in ~80% of cases (Centor criteria for strep assessment)
2. Recommend symptomatic care: paracetamol, fluids, throat lozenges
3. Advise doctor visit if: fever >38.5°C for >48h, difficulty swallowing, white patches on tonsils
4. Document the interaction

## Section 1.4 — Pharmacy team science
## القسم 1.4 — علم فريق الصيدلة

### 1.4.1 Human factors and communication

**Swiss Cheese Model (James Reason, 1990):**

Medication errors occur when "holes" in multiple system layers align:
- Layer 1: Organizational (understaffing, no double-check policy)
- Layer 2: Supervisory (inadequate training)
- Layer 3: Preconditions (fatigue, distraction, look-alike packaging)
- Layer 4: Active failure (selecting wrong drug)

**Pharmacy application:** Error prevention requires fixing **system holes**, not just blaming individuals.

### 1.4.2 SBAR communication tool

| Letter | Meaning | Pharmacy example |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Situation | "I have a prescription for warfarin 5mg for Mr. Ahmed" |
| **B** | Background | "He is also buying ibuprofen OTC for back pain" |
| **A** | Assessment | "This is a major bleeding risk interaction" |
| **R** | Recommendation | "I recommend contacting his doctor before dispensing" |

### 1.4.3 Scope of practice by role

| Role | Education (typical) | Legally can | Cannot |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pharmacist (صيدلي) | B.Pharm / Pharm.D (5 years) | Dispense Rx, clinical checking, counseling, controlled drugs | Diagnose, prescribe (except limited formulary in some countries) |
| Pharmacy assistant | Diploma / on-job training | OTC sales under supervision, stock management | Final Rx verification, controlled drug dispensing |
| Cashier | General | Payment processing | Any clinical or dispensing activity |

## Section 1.5 — Evidence-based pharmacy practice
## القسم 1.5 — ممارسة الصيدلة القائمة على الأدلة

### 1.5.1 Hierarchy of evidence

| Level | Type | Reliability |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Systematic reviews & meta-analyses of RCTs | Highest |
| 2 | Individual randomized controlled trials (RCTs) | High |
| 3 | Cohort / case-control studies | Moderate |
| 4 | Case series / case reports | Low |
| 5 | Expert opinion, mechanistic reasoning | Lowest |

**Example:** Recommending paracetamol as first-line for mild pain is **Level 1 evidence** (supported by Cochrane reviews). Recommending a specific herbal product may be **Level 4–5** only.

### 1.5.2 Critical appraisal basics

When evaluating a drug claim, ask:
1. **Validity** — Was the study well-designed? Randomized? Blinded?
2. **Importance** — Is the effect size clinically meaningful? (NNT, NNH)
3. **Applicability** — Does it apply to THIS patient in Egypt?

**NNT (Number Needed to Treat):** Number of patients who must receive treatment for one to benefit.
- Statins for primary prevention: NNT ≈ 50–100 over 5 years (moderate-risk patients)
- Antibiotics for strep throat: NNT ≈ 6–15 to prevent complications

## Section 1.6 — Practical workshop
## القسم 1.6 — ورشة عمل عملية

### Exercise 1.1 — DRP identification

Review this scenario and identify all Drug-Related Problems:

> *Patient: 68-year-old male. Medications: Metformin 500mg BD, Glibenclamide 5mg OD, Lisinopril 10mg OD, Ibuprofen 400mg TDS PRN, Aspirin 100mg OD. Labs: eGFR 42 mL/min. Complains of ankle swelling and dizziness.*

| # | DRP | Category | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Glibenclamide in CKD stage 3b | P3/P5 | Sulfonylurea hypoglycemia risk increases as eGFR declines; prefer gliclazide or DPP-4i |
| 2 | Ibuprofen TDS regular with CKD | P3/P6 | NSAIDs reduce GFR further, increase BP, risk AKI — contraindicated eGFR <30, caution <60 |
| 3 | Ibuprofen + Aspirin | P7 | Increased GI bleeding risk — COX-1 competition |
| 4 | Lisinopril + Ibuprofen | P7 | NSAIDs reduce ACE inhibitor antihypertensive effect; hyperkalemia risk |
| 5 | Ankle swelling + dizziness | P6? | May indicate fluid retention (NSAID) or hypotension (lisinopril) — needs investigation |
