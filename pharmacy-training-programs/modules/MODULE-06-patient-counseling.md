## Section 6.1 — Adherence science

### 6.1.1 WHO adherence statistics

| Finding | Data | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Adherence in chronic disease (average) | **50%** | WHO, 2003 |
| Adherence in hypertension | 50–70% | Ho 2008 meta-analysis |
| Adherence in diabetes | 40–60% | Krass 2011 |
| Adherence in depression | 50–60% | Sansone 2012 |
| Cost of non-adherence (USA) | **$100–300 billion/year** | Iuga 2014 |
| Hospitalization risk with poor adherence | **2.5× higher** | Ho 2008 |

### 6.1.2 Reasons for non-adherence (WHO 5 dimensions)

| Dimension | Examples | Pharmacy intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **Socioeconomic** | Cost, transport, low health literacy | Generic alternatives, delivery, pictorial aids |
| **Healthcare system** | Long waits, poor pharmacist communication | Efficient service, counseling time |
| **Condition-related** | Asymptomatic HTN/DM — no perceived need | Explain silent disease consequences |
| **Therapy-related** | Complex regimens, side effects | Simplify timing, manage side effects |
| **Patient-related** | Forgetfulness, denial, depression | Pill organizers, reminders, apps |

### 6.1.3 Evidence-based adherence interventions

| Intervention | Effect size (Cochrane reviews) | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Simplified dosing (once daily vs TDS) | Adherence ↑ 10–15% | Recommend sustained-release when appropriate |
| Patient education + counseling | Adherence ↑ 10–20% | Every dispensing contact |
| Pill organizers / blister packs | Adherence ↑ 5–10% | Elderly, polypharmacy patients |
| SMS/mobile reminders | Adherence ↑ 5–15% | Tech-savvy patients |
| Multicompartment aids | Adherence ↑ 15% in elderly | Weekly pill boxes |

## Section 6.2 — Counseling methodology

### 6.2.1 The WWHAM questions (UK community pharmacy standard)

| Letter | Question | Arabic |
|---|---|---|
| **W** | Who is the medicine for? | لمين الدواء ده؟ |
| **W** | What are the symptoms? | إيه الأعراض؟ |
| **H** | How long have symptoms lasted? | الأعراض من إمتى؟ |
| **A** | Action already taken? | عملت إيه قبل كده؟ |
| **M** | Medication already taking? | بتاخد أدوية تانية؟ |

### 6.2.2 Teach-back method (evidence-based)

**Protocol (AHRQ):**
1. Explain medication information clearly
2. Ask patient to **repeat back** in their own words
3. Correct misunderstandings
4. Repeat until patient demonstrates understanding
5. Document teach-back completed

**Evidence:** Kripalani et al. (2007, *Arch Intern Med*) — teach-back reduced hospital readmissions by **30%** in heart failure patients.

**Example:**
> Pharmacist: "How will you take this metformin?"
> Patient: "One tablet morning and evening with food."
> Pharmacist: "Perfect. And what will you do if you miss a dose?"
> Patient: "Take it when I remember, unless it's almost time for the next one."
> Pharmacist: "Exactly right."

### 6.2.3 Health literacy

**Single Item Literacy Screener (SILS):**
> "How often do you need someone to help you read hospital materials?"
- Never = adequate literacy
- Sometimes/Often = limited literacy → use simpler language, pictograms, Arabic only

**Impact:** Limited health literacy associated with **2× medication errors** (Davis 2006).

## Section 6.3 — Class-specific counseling monographs

### 6.3.1 Antibiotics — antimicrobial stewardship counseling

**Mandatory counseling points (WHO AWaRe classification):**

| Point | Scientific rationale |
|---|---|
| Complete the **full course** even if feeling better | Prevents selection of resistant organisms |
| Do not share antibiotics | Dose not tailored; resistance risk |
| Do not save for future use | Degraded; wrong indication |
| Most coughs/colds are **viral** — antibiotics won't help | 80% of RTIs are viral (NICE NG120) |

**Aware classification (WHO 2019):**
- **Access** (first-line): amoxicillin, metronidazole — wide availability
- **Watch** (second-line): ciprofloxacin, azithromycin — stewardship monitoring
- **Reserve** (last-resort): colistin, linezolid — hospital use primarily

### 6.3.2 Antihypertensives

| Class | Key counseling | Side effects to warn |
|---|---|---|
| ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, ramipril) | Take at same time daily; avoid K+ supplements | Dry cough (10%), angioedema (rare, serious) |
| ARBs (losartan, valsartan) | Alternative if ACE cough | Hyperkalemia |
| Calcium channel blockers (amlodipine) | Ankle swelling common, not allergic | Gum hyperplasia (rare) |
| Beta-blockers (bisoprolol) | Do not stop abruptly — rebound angina | Fatigue, cold extremities, bradycardia |
| Thiazides (hydrochlorothiazide) | Morning dosing; may increase urination | Hypokalemia, gout risk, photosensitivity |

**Home BP monitoring evidence:** Self-monitoring + pharmacist counseling reduces systolic BP by **6.1 mmHg** vs usual care (Margolis 2013, *JAMA*)

### 6.3.3 Antidiabetic agents

| Drug | Counseling | Critical safety |
|---|---|---|
| Metformin | Take with/after food; GI upset improves over 2 weeks | Stop before iodinated contrast (48h); eGFR monitoring |
| Glibenclamide/gliclazide | Take before meals; carry glucose for hypoglycemia | Hypoglycemia: sweating, tremor, confusion — take 15g fast sugar |
| Insulin | Injection rotation; storage rules; never share pens | Hypoglycemia management; HbA1c target counseling |
| SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin) | Genital mycosis risk; stay hydrated | DKA risk (euglycemic); sick day rules |
| GLP-1 agonists (liraglutide) | Injectable; nausea common initially | Pancreatitis symptoms — severe abdominal pain |

**HbA1c targets (ADA 2024):**
- Most adults: **<7%** (53 mmol/mol)
- Elderly/frail: **<8%** (64 mmol/mol)
- Each 1% HbA1c reduction = **21% reduction** in diabetes-related death (UKPDS)

### 6.3.4 Inhalers — device-specific technique

**Metered-dose inhaler (MDI) — 8 steps (GINA guidelines):**
1. Shake 5 times
2. Exhale fully
3. Lip seal around mouthpiece
4. Press canister while inhaling slowly and deeply
5. Hold breath 10 seconds
6. Wait 30 seconds between puffs
7. Use spacer in children and elderly (improves lung deposition 2–3×)
8. Rinse mouth after corticosteroid inhalers (prevents oral candidiasis)

**Error rate:** Up to **70%** of patients use inhalers incorrectly without training (Restrepo 2011). Pharmacist demonstration improves technique in **80%** of patients.

## Section 6.4 — Documentation — SOAP format

| Section | Content | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **S** (Subjective) | Patient reports | "Patient reports dizziness since starting new BP medication" |
| **O** (Objective) | Observations | "BP measured 110/70, patient appears alert" |
| **A** (Assessment) | Clinical judgment | "Likely orthostatic hypotension from amlodipine 10mg" |
| **P** (Plan) | Action | "Counseled to rise slowly; advised to contact physician if persists; teach-back completed" |
